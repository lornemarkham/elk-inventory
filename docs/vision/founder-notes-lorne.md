## The First User

I am the first user of this system. I am also the builder.

This is intentional. The best personal productivity tools get built by people who feel the pain they're solving. I feel this pain daily.

The system does not need to serve anyone else right now. It needs to serve me. When it genuinely works for me — when it saves me real time, prevents real frustration, and creates real calm — then it has a foundation worth sharing.

Build for real use first. Pricing and market positioning come later.

## What I Am Trying to Optimize

I want to be a high-performer across multiple domains simultaneously without burning out and without sacrificing time with the people and activities that matter.

In practice, that means:

- Shipping real work at a high level
- Being present with family and marriage
- Making time for parents while I still have them
- Being there for my niece as she grows up
- Riding dirt bikes and being genuinely capable on them
- Running ELK Garden as a serious, well-engineered project
- Managing a pool without it becoming a full-time job
- Keeping a tight, organized garage that enables projects — not one that swallows them
- Learning continuously: electronics, AI, systems, engineering, mechanics
- Managing property without it managing me
- Eventually building out pantry resilience without it becoming an obsession

The common thread: **I want to do all of these things well, and I want the friction between intention and doing to be as small as possible.**

ELK Inventory is a significant piece of that. When I know where my tools are, I don't spend 20 minutes finding them before I can start. When I know what I have, I don't drive to Home Depot for something already in the garage. When I know what's blocking a project, I can unblock it before the weekend.

## What I Am Not Trying to Do

I am not trying to track every object I own.

I do not need to track:
- Every screwdriver
- Every pair of scissors
- Every obvious daily-use item that's always where it should be
- Every consumable that doesn't matter if it runs out

The mental model: **track what you would actually notice if it were missing, misplaced, or lost.**

Categories of things worth tracking:
- **Expensive items** — if it's over $100, I probably want to know where it is
- **Project-critical items** — the one part that, if missing, stops the whole project
- **Seasonal items** — things used once or twice a year that I consistently forget I own
- **Hard-to-find items** — specialty parts, specific components, things with part numbers
- **Consumables with expiry** — anything that goes bad, runs out, or needs rotation
- **Safety-critical items** — first aid, fire extinguishers, the jump starter in the truck
- **Lent items** — tools or equipment borrowed by others
- **Items with unknown location** — things I know I own but can't find

If it doesn't fit one of those categories, it probably doesn't need to be in the system.

## On Inventory Scope and Intention

Inventory scope is personal and context-dependent. The same person may want to track their pantry in enormous detail (every can of beans with an expiry date) while only tracking their garage tools at a high level (power tools and diagnostic gear, not hand tools).

The right level of detail depends on the goal.

If my goal is **30-day food resilience**, I need calorie counts, expiry dates, protein sources, water storage volumes, and daily consumption estimates. Tracking individual tools doesn't serve that goal.

If my goal is **dirt bike maintenance independence**, I need the specific oil spec, filter part numbers, spark plug gaps, and a torque wrench. Tracking canned goods doesn't serve that goal.

**The system should ask: what are you trying to be capable of? Then define what inventory matters.**

This is a key insight that should shape how the system evolves. It is not a generic "track all your stuff" tool. It is a capability-supporting tool that happens to use inventory as its data model.

## On Calm and Performance

Prepared people are calmer. This is not a soft concept — it is measurable.

When I know the shop vac is in the pool shed, I don't spend cognitive energy wondering where it is. When I know I have enough oil for an oil change, I don't make a store trip mid-project. When I know the garden irrigation system is fully inventoried and documented, I don't wake up at 3am wondering if a valve failed.

That cognitive load adds up. A well-organized system takes that weight and puts it somewhere permanent — in the app, not in my head.

The payoff is mental space. Space for thinking about actual problems. Space for being present with family. Space for learning. Space for creative work.

**Organization is a performance multiplier.** It does not just save time — it improves the quality of the time that remains.

## On Monetization

Monetization is not the current priority.

I am not optimizing for MRR, growth, or acquisition right now. I am optimizing for real usefulness to real me.

That said, I am aware that:

1. The underlying problem is universal — almost everyone has friction in their physical world
2. The market for self-reliance, preparedness, and organized living is growing
3. There are people who would pay for this if it genuinely worked

When the time comes to think about monetization:
- The product will have been proven by real daily use
- I will understand the actual pain points from first-person experience
- I will know what features matter most
- I will have a credible "I built this for myself" founding story

Possible future tiers, thought through very lightly:
- Free tier: local inventory, basic search, one domain
- Personal Pro: AI intake, fuzzy search, multiple domains, capability goals
- Household / Family: shared inventory, multiple devices
- Homestead / Property: more domains, integrations, harvest tracking, larger asset register

Do not build toward these tiers right now. Do not architect around them prematurely. Build the personal use case until it is genuinely excellent, then revisit.

## On the System Creating Calm, Not More Admin

There is a real risk with systems like this: they become the thing you have to manage instead of the thing that manages things for you.

If adding an item to inventory takes 5 minutes, people won't do it. If reviewing the system requires a weekly ritual, it won't stick. If the app shows you anxiety-inducing lists of everything you're missing, it defeats the purpose.

Design principles to prevent this:
- **Capture should be fast.** Under 60 seconds for a basic item. Phone in hand, quick photo, done.
- **Enrichment is optional.** The first pass is name, photo, rough zone. Details come later if at all.
- **The app should surface what's actionable.** Not everything at once. What matters now.
- **Don't audit — assist.** The system should offer "here's what you're missing for the dirt bike project" without requiring you to manually run a checklist.
- **Nothing needs to be perfect.** An imperfect record is better than no record. A lost photo is better than a missed item.

The goal is to feel lighter after using the app, not heavier.

## Things I Keep Frustration About That This Should Solve

Honest list, in no particular order:

- Buying something on Amazon and not knowing where it ended up
- Starting a weekend project and discovering a missing part on Sunday afternoon
- Spending 20 minutes finding a specific tool that has no permanent home
- Not knowing whether to buy something locally or wait for an order
- Not knowing what food we have beyond the obvious
- Pool chemicals running low without realizing until I need them
- Forgetting that a project was blocked on a specific part
- Not knowing where a past purchase ended up
- Lend a tool and never track it
- Finding a "mystery part" and not knowing what it's for or where it came from
- Starting to build something for ELK Garden and realizing a sensor is already burned out in a bin somewhere
- Not knowing what the garden node inventory looks like when planning the next expansion

Every one of these is a real friction loop. ELK Inventory should close them, one at a time.

## Current Honest Assessment (June 2026)

What's working:
- The concept is right
- The garage domain makes sense as a starting point
- The data model is solid and extensible
- localStorage + CRUD is good enough to start using it for real
- The photo workflow is proven
- The documentation is now in the repo

What's not yet working:
- I don't actually use it daily yet — that's the next thing to change
- No mobile quick-add flow
- No purchase tracking
- No pantry domain
- No capability goals
- Photos are incomplete for many items
- Search is not fuzzy enough to be fast in the field

The goal for the next 60 days: use the app every week. Add items. Trust the system. Find out where it breaks down in real use. Fix those things before adding new domains.
